Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) is a challenging disease state

Adult B-ALL is a rare and serious disease{{CitationNumber[1]}}

● There are around 6,070 case of ALL diagnosed annually in the US{{CitationNumber[2]}}
● Most cases have an unknown etiology{{CitationNumber[1]}}

About 80% to 90% of previously untreated patients will achieve complete remission, but 44% of these patients will relapse{{CitationNumber[1]}},{{CitationNumber[3]}}

● The standard treatment for relapsed B-ALL is to induce a second complete remission (CR2) with salvage therapy and then proceed to hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) therapy{{CitationNumber[4]}}

The NCCN ALL Panel recommends that patients be treated at a specialized cancer center with expertise in the management of ALL.{{CitationNumber[5]}}

ALL is difficult to manage.{{CitationNumber[6]}},{{CitationNumber[7]}}

● B-ALL patients have a complex range of genetic abnormalities that influence outcomes and response
● A variety of genetic mutations are likely to be involved in the pathogenesis of B-ALL

Learn more about adult ALL >

 
 

ALL treatment regimens are complex and require supportive care measures

The NCCN ALL Panel recommends that patients be treated at a specialized cancer center with expertise in the management of ALL{{CitationNumber[5]}}

Specialized centers for B-All

● Treat a high volume of ALL patients
● Generally perform at least 10 transplants each year and have a dedicated transplant team of specialists and nurses{{CitationNumber[8]}}
● The transplant team is trained in the performance and management of hematopoietic transplants{{CitationNumber[8]}}

Learn more about the NCCN guidelines >

 
 

Minimal residual disease (MRD) is a prognostic indicator

MRD status is a key early warning of clinical relapse{{CitationNumber[5]}}

Relapse may be the result of the continued presence of MRD in patients, despite achieving a hematologic complete remission{{CitationNumber[9]}}

Heterogeneity is a major reason that ALL is difficult to manage{{CitationNumber[6]}},{{CitationNumber[7]}}

● MRD testing can uncover leukemic progenitor cells in patients with a hematologic complete response{{CitationNumber[5]}}
● Evaluation of bone marrow by microscopic morphological testing cannot identify the presence of leukemic cells if there are fewer than 5% (1 in 20) in the total cell population{{CitationNumber[10]}},{{CitationNumber[11]}}
● A substantial proportion of persistent MRD has been reported to consist of slowly dividing progenitor cells that may be resistant to certain therapies{{CitationNumber[7]}},{{CitationNumber[12]}}

MRD testing is routinely performed at specialized treatment centers{{CitationNumber[5]}}
